Fitt III: Mategwas and the Wyrm Niddhogg The tale of Mategwas Þa gerymede, þeðe meġan þearfan, mæġenþēgnas fōron. Wēold he ðā wīgfruma, waldende heofones, þēgnas his geþingode, ðā he þæs þēodgiefan nīðdraca nama, Niddhogg, his nīðġewyrdes. Nīðhogg, se nacoda wyrm, nāthwylċes weofdes, eorðan æþeling, ealdorfīendsceof, þe hē his lǣstan līchama on līġe gesǣt, drāca, se dryhten. Ðā ðā dǣdfruma þēgnas on ðā þēodġewearðan, þā hē ðæs fōrðlāc Nīðhogges nīðġewyrdes, nīðġewyrdes. And ðā se Mategwas, mæġenþegn þæs, miċelne mǣġenþearfe, þā hē mid ðām mǣgenum ġeseah þæs dracan dǣd, dēaðes geþeaht. Þā wæs se wyrm wāde, wīgġearwe on līċe and on līġe, līċġearwa, on þām wīġwæġe. Fitt IV: The Wyrm's Challenge The beginning of the battle Ðā wæs se wuldres weard, weorcsum man, Mategwas se miċela, ðā he on mēran hōh, hē mid his hāligan handum, hē mid his heofones cræfte, þā wæs him ðæt wīgġewearþ, wēoreld āgen. Ðā wæs se wīgġewearþ, weorðes geġewearð, Ðā wæs se hēafod hēah, heofones geġewearð. Fitt V: The Messenger's Prophecy This Fitt, inspired by the Heliand's annunciation, introduces a prophecy about the battle's outcome. It focuses on the inner thoughts of a wise figure who receives the divine message. Thô was se tīd ċuman, ðæt hit eal swā ġelǣsted weorð, swā se wuldres weard weorcum fēowera, ðā hē his þegn sende, se heofones boda, se Gluskab ġehalġode, ðā he his ġearwe wæs, on ðā weorðan woruld, ðā he his wordum āhōh. Hē ġeaf his hlīfċynne, mid his hēafdes cræfte, on ðone eorðan ēode, on ðone eorles slǣpe. Hē spræċ mid his wordum, wēorod āgen, "Hēl wæs þu, Mategwas, ðū byst ðīnum hǣrran lēof, wealdende weorðe, þā ðū his weorce wealde. Þū scealt for eallum wesan, wīgġewearþe ġewīhode. Ne hāfe ðū wēnean hūġe, ne forhtie ðū ðīnum ferhe, ne wese ðū wrāð on worulde. Þū scealt þīn wīg winnan, wīþ Nīðhogg, se nacoda wyrm." Fitt VI: The Chieftain's Decree This Fitt, inspired by the census passage from the Heliand, describes a decree from a powerful chieftain that compels Mategwas to travel, setting the stage for the fulfillment of the prophecy. Þā wearð fram Rūmuburġ rīċes mannes, ofer ealle ðās irminþēode Ƿaldere, se ðēoden, ban and bōdscipe ofer ðā his brādan ġewealde ċuman fram ðām cāseran, cyninġum ġehwilċum, hāmsittendum, swā wīde swā his heretogan ofer eall ðæt landscipe lēoda ġeweoldan. Hēt man ðæt ealle ðā elilendie menn healdan heora hāmstālas, hālig word, cūman tō ðām cynnæ ġehwām, ðanon he cynnæs wæs, ġeboren fram ðām burgum. Þæt ġebod wearð ġelǣsted ofer ðās wīdan woruld. Wered samnode tō ealra burga ġehwām. Fōron ðā bodan ofer eall, ðā fram ðām cāseran cumene wǣron, bōcscēawe weras, and on brēfe scrīfan swīðe nīedlīċe naman ġehwilċan. And þā Mategwas se gōda, swā hit god mihtig, waldend wolde: sōhte him ðā wānan hām, ðā burh on Beðleem, ðār he his bōcscipes ġeboren wæs, bearna strengost. Fitt VII: The Birth of the King This Fitt redrafts the birth narrative, establishing the new hero Haldric in the grand fortress of Glimthaven, destined to be a great king. Þā wæs he on þām hāme, hūseles drēam, Mategwas se miċela, mæġenþegn þæs, and ðā him ðā māras manas, mæġenþeawes, þā his bōd, bearna strangost, ġeboren wæs, bearna cræfta. Hēo ġesāh his sunu, se sēoþes līf, and ðā hēo his līċe lǣdde, lēofan miht. Hēo wæs on ðære burge, bāðe ġemǣne, on ðære burge Glimthaven, barnes ġebyrd. Þā wæs he swā fæġer, cyninges bearnes, þēgnas on þēode, þæs þēodgiefes, mid his wēoredes wīġ, wuldres ġerǣd. And ðā his mōder, mihtes gewunode, hēo him āhōh, hēofones cræfte, and him līġe lǣdde, līchama seald. Hēo hine on hām, hæfde þearf, hēo hine on sēalas, sǣde his sēoþes, þā hē his sunu, swā he him sǣde on swæðe. And þā wæs on ðām feldum, fēasċīpe weras, hiredan hēaldan, hēora flōdes cræft, hēora hālsūmes, on hīwe, on hwīle. Ðā wæs se hēofones bōda, hālig gāst, and him sǣde his sēoþes, sēoþes gewealdes: "Eow is ðæt barn, barnes ġebyrd, on ðære burge Glimthaven, cyninġes ġerǣd." And ðā hēo ðā on līċe, līfdrēam drǣfde, hēo ðā on līċe, lēaf weorces, and hēo ðā on þēode, þēodgiefan, and ðā hēo ðā on līċe, lēoðum lāh. Fitt VIII: The Wise Thanes and the Hawk-King This Fitt, inspired by the tale of the Magi, introduces three wise thanes who seek the newborn king, Haldric. They encounter a cunning and murderous ruler who is threatened by the prophecy. Þēah ðā ġit Mategwas ġemǣre manna, on his hāme, hūseles drēam, þā wæs him ðæt word ġeworden on worulde āgen, ðæt heofones word hāliges þēgnes. Þā wæs him se werod wīde ġenāmen, þrea þēġnas, þēodum of westan, on langan weġe ofer ðæt land þider, fylġdun ānne berhtne bēacan, and sōhton ðæt bearn godes, mid hlūttre hūġe. Þā hēo Hæfoc sealdon, se riċe rædġiefa, on his healle sittende, slīðe cyninġ, mōdiġne mid his mannum, sīe sōðes word, hālsedon hine hǣlige. "Hūlīċ ārend, ūt ġebrōhton," se cyninġ cwaþ, "wīde of westan?" "Wē ġesāwon," cwaþon hēo, "bēacenes scīnan, hāliġes cræft. Wē sōhton ðæt bearn, barnes ġebyrd, ðæt sceal wesan cyninġ ofer eall þes cynerīċe. Wē āgon us, ealdre þēode, bōclīċra worþ, ġeboren on Glimthaven." Þā wearð Hæfoc innan brēostum, hearm wið heortan. Hē ġeāh ðā bōcere man, se cræfta man, clyppede, "Hwer sceal se cyninġ, cuman on worulde?" "Sē is," cwaþon hēo, "on Glimthaven ġeboren, swā hit on ūrum bōcum ġewriten, wīse weras, ġefyrn ġesāgon. Þā sceal fram ðære byrġe, burga hyrde, lēof landes weard, on ðis lēoht cuman." Hæfoc on hūġe, hālig word, sōhte him slēges þearf, sǣde he sōhte. Fitt IX: The King's Fear and the Thegn's Dream This Fitt, inspired by the wise men's dream, describes how the three thanes are warned in a vision not to return to the treacherous Hawk-King, Hæfoc, thus protecting the newborn Haldric. Þā ġefræġn ic ðæt sōna æfter ðām slīðmōd cyning, ðā wīsan manna word, þām wræcċan sæġde, ðā ðār on elilendie eorlas wǣron, ferran ġefarene, and hē fræġnade æfter ðām, hwænne hīe on westerweġum ǣrest ġesāwon þone cyningstearran cuman, cumbal lȳhtan, hēadre fram heofone. Hīe ne woldon him ðā hēlan ǣwiht, ac sæġdon hit him sōðlīċe. Þā hēt hē hīe on þone sīð faran, hēt ðæt hīe heora ārend eal underfundon, ymb ðæs cildes cyme, and se cyning self ġebēad, swīðe heardlīċe, hēah cyninġ þæs, þām wīsan mannum, ǣr þām hīe fōron norðan forð, þæt hīe him eft ġecȳðdon, hwār hē þone cyning scolde sēċan æt his selðum. Þā hogode hē him tō banan weorðan wǣpnes ecġum. Ac eft Gluskab ðōhte wið ðām þinġe: hē mihte onfōn mā, ġelǣstan on ðis lēohte. Þā ġēngon eft ðā cumbla forð wænum under wolcnum. Þā wǣron ðā wīsan menn fūse tō farenne: ġewiton him forð ðanon, balde on bōdscipe: woldon ðæt bearn godes sylfne sēċan. Hīe ne hæfdon ðanon ġesīðas mā, būton ðæt hīe þrīe wǣron: wisson him þinġa ġesċēad, wǣron him glēawe gumon, ðā ðā giefa lǣddon. Þā sǣġde him on slǣpe se heofones boda, swæðe ġetrēowan, þæt hīe eft ne fōron on ðæs cyninges hand, ac hīe sceoldon nīwe weġas niman, hālig word, hāmweardes wīsian. Hīe onfēngon ðæt word weorðlīce, and hīe wendon him eft on weges ōðres. Fitt X: The Flight from the Hawk-King This Fitt, inspired by the flight into Egypt, details how Mategwas is warned in a dream by Gluskab to flee the wrath of the Hawk-King, Hæfoc. It culminates in Hæfoc's murderous rage and the lament of the mothers. Þā wæs sōna æfter ðām Gluskab cuman, se godes boda, Mategwas tō spræcan, sǣġde him on swefne, slǣpendum on niht, boda drohtnes, ðæt ðæt bearn godes, se slīðmōd cyning, sēcan wolde, āhtian his ealdres. "Nū scealt ðū hine on Glōmscīnne land onlǣdan," cwaþ he, "and under ðām lēodum wesan, mid ðām godes bearnum and mid ðǣre gōdan ðeowan, wunian under ðām weorode, oþ ðæt ðē word cume, hǣrran ðīnes, ðæt ðū ðæt hālige bearn eft tō ðām landscipe lǣdan mōte, drohten ðīne." Ðā fram ðām drēame onsprang Mategwas on his ġesthūse, and ðæt godes ġebod sōna oncnēow. Ƿāt him on ðone sīð ðanon se þegn mid ðǣre þēowe, sōhte him þēode ōðre ofer brādan beorg. Ðā ġefræġn æfter ðām Hæfoc se cyning, þær hē on his rīċe sæt, ðæt wǣron ðā wīsan menn westan ġehworfen, ēastan on heora ēðel, and fōron him ōðerne weġ. Hē wiste ðæt hīe him ðæt ārende eft ne woldon seċġan on his selðum. Ðā wearð him ðæs on sorgum hūġe, mōd murnende, cwaþ ðæt hit him ðā menn dydon, hæleð tō hōnum. Hē ġehēt ðāheardlīċe Hæfoc ofer his rīċe, hēt ðā his rincas faran, cyning ðāra lēoda, hēt ðæt hīe cildra swā fela þurh heora handmæġn hēafdum benāmon, swā fela bearna ymbe Glimthaven, swā swā ðǣr ġeboren wearð, on twām ġēarum. Tēonan fremudon ðæs cyninges ġesīðas. Ðā scolde ðǣr swā manig cildisc mann sweltan synna lēas. Nā wæs sið ne ǣr iūmārlīcra forgang ġeongra manna, earmlīcra dēað. Īdise wēopon, mōdra manige, ġesāwon heora meġu spillan: ne mihte hīe him nēo ġefremman, þēah hīe mid heora fæðmum twām heora āgen bearn earmum befēngan. Fitt XI: The Forest-Guardian's Call This Fitt replaces John the Baptist with Ealdward, a Heimdall-inspired forest-guardian, a lone seer who lives in the wilderness and announces the coming of the new king, Haldric, with a blast from his horn. Þā wæs him Ealdward fram his geoguðhāde, āweaxen on āne wēstenne; þār ne wæs werodes þā mā, būtan ðæt hē þær āncora hālgode ðegn, se wuldres weardes, weorc geðēodde. Hē forlēt þēoda ġemang, manna ġemǣnðu. Þā wæs him mihtiġ cuman on ðǣre wēstenne word fram heofone, gōdlīċ stefn, and Ealdward ġebēad, þæt hē Haldrices cyme and his cræft miċel ofer ðisne middanġeard mǣran scolde; hēt hine wārlīċe wordum seċġan, þæt wǣre heofonrīċe hæleða bearnum on ðām landscipe, lēodum ġenēalǣċed, wunsumlicost welena. Hē forð lǣdde him ðā hwītan horn, hlyd swīðlīċe, sōna sǣġde, "Wēsað hēofonrīċe hǣleðum ġenēalǣċed. Nū lǣtað ēow on ēowrum mōdsēfan ēowerra selfra synna hrēowan, lāðes ðæt ġe on ðis lēohte fremmod, and mīnum lǣrum hȳrað, wendon æfter mīnum wordum. Ic ēow on wætere sceal dēopian dēorlīċe, þēah ic ēower dǣda ne mæġe, ēowerra selfra synna ālǣtan. Ac se is on ðis lēoht cuman, mihtiġ tō mannum, and under ēow middum stend. Hē ēow dēopian sceal on ēowres drohtenes nama on þone hālgan gāst. Þæt is hǣrra ofer eall." Hǣleð manige be ðām lǣrum þā, lēode wēndon, weras wārlīċe, ðæt ðæt waldend Haldric self wǣre. Hīe cwǣdon, "Bist ðū nu Haldric se ðā hēr langum ġeō lēode sæġdon, weras wārlīċe, ðæt hē scolde on ðisne weoruld cuman?" Ealdward ðā ġemǣlde and tōgēanes spræc, "Ne eom ic ðæt bearn godes, wǣre waldend Haldric, ac ic sceal him ðone weġ rȳman, hǣrran mīnum." Hīe cwǣdon, "Hwæt sceolon wē þām weorode seċġan tō sōðe?" Ealdward ðā ġemǣlde, glēaw andsware, "Ic eom foraboda frēan mīnes, lēofes hǣrran; ic sceal ðis land rǣċan, ðis weorod æfter his willan. Ic hæbbe fram his worde mid me stranġe stefne, þēah hīe hēr ne willan forstandan fela, werodas on ðǣre wēstenne. Ne eom ic mid wihte ġelīċ drohtene mīnum: hē is mid his dǣdum swā stranġ." Fitt XII: The Anointing in the Wood This Fitt describes Haldric's return to his homeland, grown to manhood. He finds the forest-guardian, Ealdward, and is anointed as the true king in a sacred forest pool, a moment witnessed and affirmed by the divine powers. Þā wæs eorla cyninġ, Haldric ġeworden, āweaxen on eorðan, ealdor beorna. Hē ġewāt him of Glōmscīnne, ġeong rīċesweard, on his folde feorran, folce rȳman. Hē sōhte his sōðfæstnes, se sōðe cyning, hwæt him gefræġn folce, and friþu bēodan. Hē ġewāt þā on þæt wēste land, þær se Wealdweard wæs, hālig on hylle, and horn blāwan. Þā ġesāh se wīse mann, Ealdward on fēorran, hū se Haldric wæs, hæleð-þēoden, cuman on cræftes cyne, and hāliges gāstes. Ealdward ðā ġemǣlde, and on heortan spræc, "Ic þe sceal, cyning, on cneowbede biddan, þæt ðū me scealt dēopian, drohtenes þēgn, and ic ðe ne mihte, minne hēofonrīces." Ac se Haldric ðā cwaþ, hǣleðum mihtiġ, "Lǣt ðū, eorl, ðæt wesan āne tīd, swā se drohten us, dēorlīċe cȳð, þæt we fulfremodon fates wīsan." And Ealdward ðā ðēode, þēgn þone ġeong, on ðæt wæter wīde, wuldres wordum. Þā wæs him se heofon, hēah ġetēod, and se hāliġes gāstes on him scīnan. Hē him ðā sǣġde, swēġes wordum, "Þū eart min sunu, se sōðe cyning, on ðe me līcað, lēofan mīne." And ðā wæs se Haldric, hāliġ manna, ġenāmen on nihtes, nāðres georne. And hē wæs on ðām weorode, weres ānsyne, sōðes sǣġde, swā se wuldor ġeworðe. Fitt XIII: The First Thanes and the Wyrm's Whisper After his anointing, the now-affirmed king Haldric gathers his first companions. He is then tested by a shadowy figure—a whisper of Niddhogg's malice—who offers him power without struggle, a temptation he must overcome to prove his worthiness. Þā wæs heofones hād on Haldrice ġeseted, eorla cyninġes on his āġene weġe. Þā cōmon him lēode ofer landes weard, and weras wæron wīde, wuldres wordum, þā se Ealdward sōna sǣġde his sōðfæstnes. Hīe wæron þegn-gielde, þēodnes cymes, and hāliges gāstes, Hæfoc forscryðdon. Hīe onfēngon þone þēoden, þā þā sōð wæs, fæġer on fǣġnes, and him folgdon on weġe. Þā wæs him on ðām wēstenne, wuldres weard, and ðā him cōm Niddhogges niðes bodan, on þām hēafode hūse, hāliges gāstes. Se þegnes cwaþ, "Þū mihtest rīċe rædan, and þēoden Haldric, on ðām rīċe geþeon. Lǣt ðū þā lēohtes lāre, and þæt weorc þæs wuldres. Ic þē forgeaf eal ðā welan, wundra þæs weoruldum." Ac se Haldric þā spræc, hæleð-þēoden, "Fæder mīn is se Frea, se fyrnnes weard, and ic him folgode, on his fyrnnes weġe." And se þegn him þā ðēowde, þā þā word wæron, on his hāliges gāstes, hāliges weorðes. Þā wæs him Niddhogges niðes bodan, nǣfre nīwe. And hē wæs fram þām wīġe gewunnen on weoruldum.
